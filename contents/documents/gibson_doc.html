Gibson Assembly Primer Designer

The Gibson Assembly module enables the seamless assembly of multiple DNA fragments in a single isothermal one-pot reaction through rational primer design with complementary overlap regions. This tool automates the creation of primers with optimized overlap sequences (typically 20–30 bp) that allow exonuclease, polymerase, and ligase enzymes to assemble DNA fragments without relying on restriction sites. Gibson Assembly is particularly well-suited for rapid prototyping, constructing large multi-fragment constructs, and flexible cloning where restriction sites may not be available.

Quick Start Guide

  1. Upload or paste your destination vector and insert sequences (maximum of 6 fragment) in FASTA or plain text format into the corresponding input fields, in the desired assembly order. Select the vector linearization methods by either restriction enzymes or PCR. Verify the automatically detected vector backbone, and adjust design parameters if necessary.
  2. Click "Design Primers" to generate primers with integrated overhang sequences. Review the primer table, overlap table, and predicted assembly schematic. Download the assembled FASTA file to verify that the insert orientation matches the intended design. If the orientation is incorrect, reverse-complement the insert sequence and regenerate the primers

Gibson Assembly Principle

Gibson Assembly is an isothermal, one-pot DNA assembly method that enables seamless joining of DNA fragments through homologous overlap regions without the need for restriction enzymes. In a single reaction, a 5′ exonuclease resects the ends of double-stranded DNA fragments to generate complementary 3′ single-stranded overhangs, which anneal specifically between adjacent fragments. A high-fidelity DNA polymerase then fills in the remaining gaps using the annealed 3′ ends as primers, and a thermostable DNA ligase subsequently seals the nicks in the DNA backbone. Together, these coordinated enzymatic activities result in scarless assembly of multiple DNA fragments into a continuous double-stranded DNA molecule, making Gibson Assembly particularly suitable for constructing large or complex DNA constructs.

Overlap Design Principle

The success of Gibson Assembly relies on the design of complementary overlap regions between adjacent DNA fragments. Each junction requires a pair of overlaps that are complementary in sequence and sufficient in length to support stable annealing during assembly.

  • Complementary Overlaps: The 3′ end of fragment i must be complementary to the 5′ end of fragment i + 1, ensuring specific annealing between adjacent fragments.
  • Optimal Overlap Length:Overlaps of 20–30 bp are generally sufficient for most assemblies, balancing thermal stability and primer length; longer overlaps may improve robustness for complex or large constructs.
  • Seamless Junctions: Because overlaps are derived directly from native sequence, Gibson Assembly produces scar-free junctions without introducing additional residues.

Input Format

Both vector and insert inputs accept FASTA, plain DNA text, or standard GenBank files (.gb, .gbk, .gbff, .genbank) with LOCUS and ORIGIN blocks. The short name in LOCUS is used as the sequence title. Only one sequence is allowed per input field; if multiple FASTA headers are detected, only the first sequence will be used. If you upload plain sequence without a > header, the file name (without extension) is used as the name. Proprietary binary formats (e.g. SnapGene .dna) must be exported as GenBank or FASTA first. Fragment names from FASTA or GenBank are used in primer naming where applicable.

PrimerWeaver currently supports approximately 100 built-in Type II restriction enzymes. Once a vector sequence is provided, the system automatically identifies vector features and displays the positions of all single-cut restriction enzyme sites. The vector map can be rotated to facilitate visualization of the targeted cloning region.

Fragment Management

  • Add/Remove Fragments: Add multiple DNA sequences for multi-fragment assembly (2–10 fragments recommended; larger assemblies become more complex)
  • Reorder Fragments: Adjust assembly order using interface controls; order determines final construct architecture
  • Fragment Names: Auto-extracted from FASTA headers for clear primer identification; used in output reports
  • Reverse Complement: Toggle individual fragments if needed to match desired orientation and assembly direction

Results and Output

After primer design, the Gibson Assembly module generates a comprehensive set of results to facilitate experimental validation and downstream cloning.

Primer Table

Designed primer pairs are presented in a structured table with complete primer sequences information (5′→3′), length, GC content, and melting temperatures (Tm). Tm values are displayed for both the annealing core region and the full-length primer, allowing users to assess amplification compatibility. Primer quality metrics, including homopolymer runs, hairpin structures, self-dimer, and cross-dimer formation, are evaluated and flagged to highlight potential risks that may affect PCR performance. Overlap regions are highlighted in bold within primer sequences for easy visualization.

Assembled diagram and Overhang table

An assembled plasmid schematic is generated to illustrate the cloning workflow, including vector linearization, insert incorporation, and final plasmid assembly. This diagram provides a conceptual overview of fragment orientation and junction structure. A overlap table displays the overlap sequence at each junction, confirming that adjacent fragments have complementary homologous region. This table provides a quick visual verification that the assembly architecture is correct and that no overhang conflicts or mismatches are present.

Note: The assembly diagram is schematic only and is not drawn to scale. Exact interpretation should be based on the assembled sequence and junction overlap information.

Simulated Agarose Gel Electrophoresis

PrimerWeaver generates a simulated agarose gel image to predict experimental outcomes for key cloning steps. The gel includes:

  • A selectable DNA ladder (default: NEB 1 kb Plus DNA Ladder)
  • Undigested vector
  • Linearized vector by digestion or PCR
  • PCR-amplified fragments (one lane per fragment, showing expected size including overlap)
  • Gibson Assembly reaction product (showing assembled construct)

Predicted band sizes are annotated alongside the gel, enabling users to compare expected fragment sizes with experimental results and quickly identify potential cloning issues.

Primer Design Algorithm

Primer Structure

Each Gibson Assembly primers consists of two functional regions:

  • Annealing core (3′): template-complementary region that drives PCR annealing (designed within an 18-40 bp search window)
  • Overlap Region (5′): Sequence copied from the adjacent junction context in the final assembly and appended as the Gibson homology tail (typically 20-30 bp).

Design Process

PrimerWeaver does not design Gibson overlaps in isolation. Instead, it first builds the actual assembly context, then extracts overlap segments directly from that context, and only then constructs full primers. In practice, the algorithm is easiest to understand as four linked stages.

Stage 1. Define the retained vector backbone and the assembled sequence

If restriction-enzyme linearization is selected, the module digests the circular vector in silico, lists all candidate fragments, and uses the user-selected backbone fragment as the retained vector piece. It then inserts the ordered insert block into that backbone context and optionally keeps or removes restriction-site sequence depending on the keep sites setting. In this mode the code also tracks a raw unrotated assembly and may rotate the displayed circular sequence so that the reported assembly is easier to interpret while still preserving the original junction coordinates for overlap extraction.

If PCR linearization is selected, the module maps the user-supplied vector primers onto the vector by anchored 3′ matching, allows reverse-complement / primer-swap fallback when needed, and chooses the best valid amplicon pair. The retained backbone sequence is then the reconstructed PCR product, and the ordered insert block is appended into that linearized backbone context to form the assembled sequence used for downstream overlap design.

Stage 2. Determine insert coordinates in the final assembly

After the vector backbone is fixed, the module calculates the start and end position of every insert in the assembled construct. In enzyme-linearization mode, these coordinates are mapped through any circular rotation applied for display; in PCR-linearization mode, they are accumulated directly along the reconstructed linear backbone. This coordinate map is critical because Gibson overlaps are taken from the assembled junction neighborhood, not guessed independently for each primer.

Stage 3. Design the annealing cores on each insert

For each insert, PrimerWeaver designs a forward core from the insert 5′ end and a reverse core from the insert 3′ end. Core lengths are scanned across an 18-40 bp window and evaluated with the nearest-neighbor Tm model. The selected core is the one that best satisfies the target Tm behavior under the current salt / primer-concentration settings; if no ideal choice is available, the algorithm falls back to the closest workable core rather than abandoning the design.

Stage 4. Extract Gibson overlaps from assembly junctions and build full primers

Once insert positions are known, the module extracts the overlap for each insert end from the assembled sequence itself. The left overlap is taken from the junction sequence immediately upstream of the insert, and the right overlap is taken from the junction sequence immediately downstream. For vector-facing ends, circular subsequence logic is used when necessary so that overlaps can cross the origin of the rotated plasmid view. For insert-insert junctions, the overlap geometry depends on whether an optional linker is long enough to supply the full overlap by itself or whether the overlap must straddle linker and neighboring insert sequence together.

Full primers are then assembled as 5′ overlap + 3′ annealing core. Forward primers receive the left overlap directly, whereas reverse primers receive the reverse complement of the right overlap before the reverse annealing core is appended. In this way, the final primer pair simultaneously amplifies each insert and installs the exact homology required by the chosen backbone, fragment order, and linker configuration.

Edge-Case Logic in the Algorithm

Algorithm edge case Current behavior in Gibson module
Linearization mode changes the whole geometry Restriction-enzyme mode and PCR mode do not just change the input form; they produce different backbone sequences, different coordinate systems, and different overlap-extraction paths. The Gibson overlap layout is therefore mode-dependent from the start.
Vector linearization cannot be defined cleanly If enzyme-linearization or PCR-linearization fails to produce a valid retained backbone sequence, the algorithm stops rather than guessing a junction architecture.
PCR-linearization orientation is ambiguous If the initial PCR-primer interpretation fails but a reverse-complement / swapped-primer interpretation succeeds, the module automatically adopts that recovered orientation instead of discarding the design immediately.
Backbone selection is required after digestion When restriction digestion yields multiple fragments, the algorithm cannot proceed until one fragment is designated as the retained backbone. Changing that selection changes the overlap coordinates and the final primer set.
Recognition-site retention changes the assembled sequence If the workflow keeps restriction-site sequence during enzyme-based linearization, the assembled backbone context changes before overlaps are extracted, so the junction homology may differ from the seamless site-free version.
Overlap design is junction-specific and backbone-aware Overlaps are not assigned as generic tails. They are cut from the assembled junction neighborhood after backbone choice, insert order, and any linker placement are fixed, so changing any of those factors changes the overlap solution.
Linker length changes overlap extraction geometry If a linker is long enough, the algorithm can take the full overlap from the linker interior. If the linker is short or absent, the overlap is split across neighboring sequence so that the final homology still spans the junction.
Rotated plasmid display does not equal raw overlap coordinates In enzyme-linearization mode, the displayed assembled plasmid may be rotated for readability, but overlap extraction still has to respect the raw pre-rotation junction coordinates so that the reported primers match the true assembly geometry.
Core search may not hit an ideal target exactly The algorithm scans a bounded core-length window and picks the closest workable Tm solution. If no perfect target-Tm hit exists, it falls back to the best available core instead of failing outright.

Warnings and Edge Cases

The Gibson Assembly module uses the shared MW (Message Warning) layer for preflight validation where applicable, and also raises module-specific design errors or design notes when vector linearization, fragment selection, or overlap generation becomes ambiguous.

General warning

These warnings are primarily generated before design starts and usually indicate input-quality or parameter-quality issues. Depending on the situation, users can either revise the setup or continue with the current settings.

Category Trigger Current behavior in Gibson module
Blocking input prompts Missing vector sequence, missing inserts, missing PCR primers in PCR-linearization mode, invalid enzyme names, or invalid backbone selection. Design stops immediately and the module reports what must be corrected before a valid design can proceed.

MW-05
Non-IUPAC characters removed		 Input contains unsupported characters that are stripped during normalization. The module proceeds with normalized sequences only and warns that the effective design template may have changed.
MW-06
Degenerate bases detected		 Vector or insert contains ambiguous IUPAC bases. Thermodynamic values are estimated conservatively from the most stable variant, so Tm and ΔG values may represent a worst-case approximation.

MW-OVERLAP-01

Practical interpretation: warnings in Gibson Assembly do not always indicate a failed design. Some warnings reflect sequence normalization, fragment-count limits, or ambiguity in vector linearization and primer orientation. Results should therefore be interpreted together with the selected backbone fragment, the overlap table, and the final assembled sequence.
